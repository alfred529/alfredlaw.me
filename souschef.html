Case study · 2026

SousChef

An AI-powered recipe collection app for iOS and Android — capturing recipes from any source and saving them as structured files. Designed solo in 10 days as an AI-assisted portfolio sprint.

Role

Solo Product Designer

Duration

10 days

Platform

iOS & Android

AI Collaborator

Claude (Anthropic)

Deliverables

Research, Design system, 8-screen prototype

Problem

Recipes live everywhere.
Nowhere useful.

Home cooks collect recipes across dozens of surfaces — YouTube videos, Instagram reels, TikTok saves, blog posts, scanned cookbook pages, screenshots. None of them are structured. None of them are searchable. None of them surface when you're standing at the stove.

The result is a spike-and-crash pattern: frantic saving when inspiration strikes, followed by recipes that are never actually cooked. Collections grow, remain inaccessible, and accumulate guilt.

✦ AI process note

The problem statement was refined iteratively with Claude. The first draft focused on organisation, but research synthesis revealed the real gap was in the capture-to-cook pipeline. That reframe shifted the entire product direction.

Research

Two users, one tension.

Research surfaced two distinct user modes: the impulsive saver who needs frictionless capture above all else, and the deliberate cook who wants to review before committing. The central design challenge was serving both without a settings toggle.

The tension resolved into a confidence-routing system — the AI agent silently routes based on extraction confidence. High confidence (≥80%) saves immediately; lower confidence surfaces a review screen. Neither user has to manage this explicitly.

Competitive Audit

The gap no one had filled.

No existing recipe app handles video transcript extraction or multi-screenshot stitching. Both became SousChef's core differentiators — gaps discovered directly through the competitive audit.

Key Decisions

Four choices that
defined the product.

Each decision represents a fork where the obvious path was rejected in favour of one grounded in research.

01 — Agent routing

Silent, confidence-based routing

The agent saves immediately at ≥80% confidence, surfaces a review screen below. Users never configure this — removing cognitive load from the capture moment entirely.

Rejected: User-configurable "always review" toggle. Added choice at the worst moment.

02 — Pipeline visibility

Named pipeline steps, not a spinner

The saving screen names each step: OCR extraction → stitching → parsing → confidence scoring → Drive save. Users reported higher trust in the output when they could see what had been done.

Rejected: Generic loading spinner. Measured increase in perceived wait time.

03 — Uncertainty display

Per-field confidence flags

Below threshold, the confirm screen flags specific uncertain fields rather than showing a global warning. Precision reduces cognitive load — users review one thing, not everything.

Rejected: Global "low confidence" banner. Caused over-correction in testing.

04 — Tag generation

Post-extraction, pre-filled and editable

Tags generated after extraction, pre-filled by AI, presented as an editable step users can skip. Starting from a correct answer beats starting from a blank form.

Rejected: Tags before save. Friction at the moment of highest save intent.

Design System

Editorial with warmth.

Built on one principle from NYT Cooking: serif for recipe content, sans-serif for UI chrome. Playfair Display handles every piece of recipe content. DM Sans handles all navigation, labels, and buttons. That single typographic split is what makes the app feel editorial rather than like another productivity tool.

Avocado green is the only action colour — appearing on primary buttons, checked ingredients, active steps, and nowhere else. Every green element is interactive or confirmed.

Screen Designs

Every screen in the flow.

From first open to active cooking. Checklists, timers, tag editing, step navigation, and overlap detection — all functional in the prototype.

✦ AI process note

All 8 screens were built as a fully interactive HTML prototype using Claude as design collaborator — approximately 35 iteration cycles across the sprint. Claude drafted initial layout structures; every design decision — typography, spacing, component choices, colour assignments, accessibility — was evaluated, redirected, and approved by the designer. The AI generated first drafts; the designer set direction.

Outcomes

What the sprint produced.

A complete research foundation, a documented design system, and a fully interactive 8-screen prototype — all with design decisions traceable back to specific research findings.

The three most important outputs weren't the screens themselves. They were the confidence-routing decision, the per-field uncertainty model, and cook mode as the direct response to the "long dormancy" insight from the journey map. Each can be validated independently in development, making the handoff unusually tractable.
